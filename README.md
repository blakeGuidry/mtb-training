# The 13-Week BC Bike Race Peak Performance Plan

A periodized training plan for the 2026 BC Bike Race (May 23–29), a 7-day mountain bike stage race across Vancouver Island, BC. This plan covers the final 13 weeks of preparation: Build 1, Build 2 / Specialty, Taper, and Race.

**Start Date:** March 2, 2026
**Race Dates:** May 23–29, 2026
**Equipment:** Wahoo Kickr Core 2 + Zwift (indoor) | Mountain Bike with Garmin Watch + HR Monitor, no power meter (outdoor)

## Section 1: Benchmarking & The "Dual-Zone" System

This plan operates on a "Dual-Zone" system, calibrating two separate sets of training zones: one based on Heart Rate (HR) for outdoor mountain bike training (which lacks a power meter) and one based on Power (FTP) for indoor Zwift training on the Kickr Core 2.

### 1.1 Protocol 1: Establishing Cycling Lactate Threshold Heart Rate (LTHR)

All outdoor ride intensities are prescribed based on Lactate Threshold Heart Rate (LTHR). LTHR is the exertion level above which fatigue accumulates rapidly, making it the most reliable anchor for setting endurance training zones.

A critical clarification: the LTHR detection features on Garmin watches (even those with "Auto Detection" or "Guided Tests") are designed for and validated by running. Lactate threshold is sport-specific; a running LTHR is physiologically different from a cycling LTHR and cannot be used interchangeably. The following 30-minute cycling-specific field test must be performed outdoors on the mountain bike using the Garmin watch and heart rate monitor.

**LTHR Field Test Protocol:**

1. **Warm-up:** Complete 15–20 minutes of easy spinning, gradually increasing intensity.
2. **Main Set:** Begin a 30-minute sustained time trial. The objective is to ride at the fastest, most consistent pace possible for the entire 30 minutes. Pacing is critical; avoid starting too hard and fading, as this will skew the results.
3. **Data Capture:** Exactly 10 minutes into the 30-minute effort, press the "Lap" button on the Garmin watch. This isolates the final 20 minutes of the test.
4. **Finish:** Complete the 30-minute time trial.
5. **Cool-down:** Perform 10–15 minutes of very easy (Zone 1) spinning.
6. **Analyze:** After saving the activity, review the data on the Garmin watch or in Garmin Connect. The LTHR benchmark is the average heart rate for the final 20-minute lap of the test.

**LTHR Retest Schedule:** Retest after Week 4 (end of Build 1) and optionally during Week 10 (Taper 1) if course conditions allow a clean test effort.

### 1.2 Protocol 2: Establishing Zwift Functional Threshold Power (FTP)

All indoor Zwift ride intensities are prescribed based on Functional Threshold Power (FTP). The Zwift Ramp Test is the recommended protocol for establishing FTP on the Kickr Core 2.

**FTP Test Protocol:**

1. **Preparation:** In Zwift, navigate to Workouts and select "FTP Tests." Choose the Ramp Test (shorter and less draining than a 20-minute test, making it suitable for recovery weeks).
2. **Test:** Complete the ramp test. The resistance increases incrementally every minute until you can no longer maintain the target power.
3. **Analysis:** Zwift automatically calculates FTP from the ramp test result (approximately 75% of the highest 1-minute power achieved).
4. **Zwift Setup:** Accept the new FTP value in Zwift. This will automatically calibrate all 7 Power Zones used in Zwift workouts.

**Important:** The FTP measured on the Kickr Core 2 will likely differ from any previous Peloton FTP. Direct-drive smart trainers and Peloton bikes measure power differently. Always use the Zwift-tested FTP for all indoor training zones.

**FTP Retest Schedule:** Test during Week 1 (this recovery week). Retest after Week 4 (end of Build 1, recovery week).

### 1.3 Training Zones

With the LTHR and FTP benchmarks established, the following two 7-zone models serve as the "Rosetta Stone" for this plan. A 7-zone model is selected over 5-zone models because it provides superior granularity, particularly in separating Zone 2 (Endurance) from Zone 3 (Tempo) — a distinction foundational for building the deep aerobic base required for stage racing.

**Table 1: Outdoor Training – 7-Zone Heart Rate (HR) Model**
(Based on the Coggan/Garmin 7-Zone %LTHR Model)

| Zone | Name | % of LTHR | RPE | Purpose | Feel |
|:-----|:-----|:----------|:----|:--------|:-----|
| **Z1** | Active Recovery | <68% | <2/10 | Promotes recovery | Easy, barely pedaling |
| **Z2** | Endurance | 69–83% | 2–3/10 | Builds aerobic base, "all-day" pace | Conversational. Full sentences. |
| **Z3** | Tempo | 84–94% | 3–4/10 | "Comfortably hard," muscular endurance | Short sentences, prefer not to talk. |
| **Z4** | Lactate Threshold | 95–105% | 4–5/10 | Increases sustained high-intensity capacity | A few words at most. Labored breathing. |
| **Z5** | VO2 Max | >106% | 6–7/10 | Boosts maximum aerobic power | Cannot talk. Counting down the seconds. |
| **Z6** | Anaerobic Capacity | N/A (HR lag) | >7/10 | Short, maximal efforts | Max effort, unsustainable beyond 30–60s. |
| **Z7** | Neuromuscular | N/A (HR lag) | Maximal | Sprints | All-out. |

*Note: HR lags power by 20–30 seconds on short efforts. For intervals under 3 minutes (Z5, Z6, Z7), rely on RPE rather than HR.*

### 1.4 Zwift Setup & ERG Mode

The Wahoo Kickr Core 2 with Zwift provides two distinct training modes. Use the correct mode for each session type:

- **ERG Mode ON:** For all structured interval workouts (threshold, VO2 max, sweet spot, microbursts). In ERG mode, the trainer automatically adjusts resistance to hold you at prescribed power targets regardless of cadence. Every second is productive training stress.
- **ERG Mode OFF:** For free rides, recovery spins, climbing routes (Alpe du Zwift, Innsbruck KOM, etc.), and low-cadence torque work. With ERG off, the Kickr Core 2 simulates gradient resistance and you control your effort through gearing and cadence, more closely replicating outdoor riding.

**Zwift Workout Sources:**

- **Zwift Workout Library:** Built-in structured workouts organized by collection (Endurance, Threshold, HIIT, Sweet Spot, Mountain Bike, Recovery, etc.).
- **Zwift Training Plans:** Multi-week structured plans (not used in this plan to avoid conflicts with the periodized schedule, but individual workouts from plans like Singletrack Slayer can be cherry-picked).
- **Zwift Workout Builder:** Create custom workouts (used for Start Simulations and Taper Openers in this plan).
- **BCBR Zwift Club:** Official BC Bike Race club with scheduled group rides (see Section 1.5).

### 1.6 Cadence Targets

Cadence is a tradeoff between cardiovascular load and muscular load. Low cadence (50–65 RPM) loads the muscles heavily but keeps heart rate lower. High cadence (100–110+ RPM) reduces force per stroke but drives heart rate up. This plan trains the full cadence spectrum because BCBR singletrack constantly forces transitions between grinding steep pitches and spinning flats. Default to 85–95 RPM for general training — this is the range where most cyclists produce the best balance of power output and metabolic efficiency.

| Session Type | Target RPM | Rationale |
|:-------------|:-----------|:----------|
| Z2 Endurance (default) | 85–95 | Best balance of power and efficiency. "All-day" cadence. |
| Sweet Spot / Threshold (ERG on) | 85–95 | Let the trainer control resistance. Focus on smooth pedal strokes. |
| VO2 Max intervals | 90–100 | Slightly higher reduces muscular fatigue, allowing more intervals before failure. |
| Low-cadence torque blocks | 50–65 | Deliberate exception. High-torque pedaling for steep, loose climbs. |
| Spin-up drills | 100–120 | 30s progressive acceleration in easy gear, Z1/Z2 power. Trains neuromuscular transition from grind to spin. |
| Microbursts (15s on/off) | Natural (~95–105) | Let cadence rise naturally during surges. Don't fight it. |
| Race day (outdoor) | 50–110+ | Terrain dictates. Training both ends prepares legs for constant transitions. |

**Spin-up Drill Protocol:** During Thursday Z2 endurance rides, include 3–4 spin-ups alongside the low-cadence blocks. Each spin-up is 30 seconds of progressively increasing cadence from 90 to 110–120 RPM in an easy gear, staying at Z1/Z2 power. This trains pedaling smoothness and the neuromuscular ability to "shift gears" from torque mode to spin mode — critical for accelerating out of steep climbs onto flats without losing momentum.

## Section 2: Race Profile & Training Targets

### 2.1 2026 BC Bike Race Course Overview

The 2026 BCBR is a 7-stage race across Vancouver Island with stages in Cumberland (2 stages), Campbell River, Parksville (new venue), Mt. Tzouhalem, Nanaimo, and Maple Mountain. Course descriptions emphasize non-stop undulating terrain, punchy rolling climbs, and technical west coast singletrack with rocks and roots.

**Key Benchmark:** ~155 ft/mi elevation density is the target profile. Training routes should aim for 130–180 ft/mi of climbing to match BCBR demands.

### 2.3 Climbing Compensation Strategy

Local terrain provides approximately 100–115 ft/mi of climbing, which is 65–75% of BCBR race density. The following strategies close this gap:

1. **Hill Repeats (Primary):** Restructure Saturday long rides around repeating local climbs rather than riding point-to-point routes. Loop the best available climbs to accumulate target elevation gain. Example: if the best local climb is 500 ft over 1.5 miles, 10 repeats yields 5,000 ft of climbing.
2. **Zwift Climbing Routes (Secondary):** On alternating Thursdays, ride Alpe du Zwift, Road to Sky, or Innsbruck KOM with ERG off. The Kickr Core 2 simulates gradient resistance, training gear selection and sustained climbing pacing.
3. **Cadence Spectrum Training (Supplementary):** All indoor Z2 endurance rides include low-cadence torque blocks (6–8 minutes at 50–65 RPM, Z2–Z3 power) and high-cadence spin-up drills (30s to 110–120 RPM). The torque blocks train the pedaling pattern for steep, loose climbs; the spin-ups train the neuromuscular transition to accelerating on flats after cresting a climb.

Do NOT compensate for low elevation density by pushing a higher HR zone on flat terrain. Riding Z4 on flat ground trains a different neuromuscular pattern than riding Z3 on a steep climb. Climbing-specific fitness comes from climbing.

### 2.4 Outdoor Ride Pacing Metrics

Track these metrics after every outdoor ride to calibrate race-day pacing:

- **Vertical Ascent Rate (VAM) by zone:** Feet climbed per hour at Z2, Z3, and Z4. Your sustainable Z2 VAM is your core race-pacing metric.
- **Time per 1,000 ft of climbing at Z3 and Z4:** This determines race-pace climbing speed.
- **Pace degradation on Day 2:** Compare Sunday VAM to Saturday VAM at the same zone during Stage Race Simulation weekends. This gap should shrink over the Build 2 weeks.
- **Average moving speed at target elevation density:** On routes matching 130–180 ft/mi, what is the average speed? Use this to estimate BCBR stage finish times.

## Section 3: The 13-Week Training Plan Structure

### 3.1 Week 1: Recovery / Transition

Following the Base 2 block (completed), this is a dedicated recovery week focused on absorbing prior training adaptations and establishing a new FTP baseline on the Kickr Core 2 / Zwift setup.

### 3.2 Weeks 2–4: Build 1 (Mesocycle 6)

With a massive aerobic base established in the prior 17 weeks, this mesocycle begins to "build the ceiling" by introducing high-intensity training. The progression uses a sweet spot bridge (Weeks 2–3: 88–93% FTP) before full Z4 threshold intervals (Week 4: 95–105% FTP). The weekend long ride incorporates sustained Z3 (Tempo) and Z4 (Threshold) efforts to simulate race-pace climbing. Low-cadence torque blocks and high-cadence spin-up drills are introduced on all indoor endurance rides. Every other Thursday features a Zwift climbing route (ERG off) to build gradient-specific fitness.

### 3.3 Week 5: Recovery

Absorb Build 1 adaptations. Recommended FTP retest.

### 3.4 Weeks 6–8: Build 2 / Specialty (Mesocycle 7)

This is the peak training block, designed to be the most difficult of the entire plan. The focus shifts to Z5 (VO2 Max) intervals during the week, midweek microburst sessions (15s on / 15s off at 150% FTP) to simulate punchy singletrack surging, and "Stage Race Simulation" blocks on the weekend.

The Stage Race Simulation is the key to this mesocycle:

- **Saturday:** A "Kitchen Sink" ride, 4–5 hours in duration, that includes a mix of all relevant intensities (Z3, Z4, and Z5 intervals). Hill repeats are used to hit elevation targets. The ride ends with 20–30 minutes of deliberate technical skills practice under fatigue.
- **Sunday:** A "Fatigued Endurance" ride, 3–4 hours in duration, performed on legs already depleted from Saturday's effort. Pace degradation is tracked relative to Saturday.

The primary adaptation from this block is enhancing the body's ability to perform on consecutive days with significant fatigue — training metabolic efficiency, glycogen sparing, and the psychological resilience required for the later stages of BCBR.

This phase also introduces optional "Start Simulation" drills: short, maximal-effort sprints (6–8 × 20s from a standing start) designed to mimic the chaotic and anaerobic start of a mountain bike race.

### 3.5 Week 9: Recovery

Absorb peak training. Begin mental transition toward taper and race execution.

### 3.6 Weeks 10–11: Taper

Following the peak training block, a 2-week taper sheds accumulated fatigue and allows the body to supercompensate.

- **Week 10 (Taper 1):** Volume is reduced by ~20–25%. A final, shortened Stage Race Sim is performed. Intensity is maintained via short "opener" workouts.
- **Week 11 (Taper 2):** Volume is reduced by 40–50%. Intensity is maintained via short opener workouts. Final bike check and equipment preparation.

### 3.7 Weeks 12–13: Race

During the 7-day BC Bike Race, all training adaptation is complete. The entire focus shifts from fitness to execution and recovery. The daily plan is to race, then immediately begin the recovery protocol to maximize performance for the following day (see Section 5).

## Section 4: Integrated Strength & Conditioning Program

Two strength phases remain active in this plan, plus an ongoing core routine. The phases are aligned with the cycling macrocycle to support, not detract from, on-bike goals. Phase 1 uses an A/B split across its two weekly sessions to provide variety and cover the full spectrum of MTB demands — concentric climbing power, eccentric descending resilience, grip endurance, and pulling strength — without bloating any single session.

### 4.1 Phase 1 – Strength Maintenance (Heavy) (Weeks 1–9)

- **Goal:** Maintain the maximal strength and neuromuscular efficiency gained during the prior Maximum Strength phase (completed), which is critical for improving cycling economy and delaying fatigue. This phase aligns with the Build 1 and Build 2 blocks.
- **Protocol:** 2 sessions per week (Session A on Tuesdays, Session B on Fridays). 3 sets of 3–6 repetitions unless noted. Load: Heavy (80–95% 1RM). Rest: 2–4 minutes between sets. Farmer's carries and wrist roller are performed at the end of the session.
- **Rationale:** Evidence strongly supports maintaining _intensity_ (heavy weight) while reducing _volume_ (sets/reps) during the build phase. High-rep "endurance" lifting is less effective as it merely replicates the metabolic stress of cycling. This protocol maintains neural drive without inducing excessive fatigue or muscle soreness.

**Table 1a: Phase 1 Session A – Climbing Power & Unilateral Strength (Tuesday)**

| Exercise | Sets | Reps | Weight | Why |
|:---------|:-----|:-----|:-------|:----|
| Barbell Squat or Leg Press | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Max leg force production for climbing. |
| Deadlift (or Trap Bar) | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Full-body posterior chain power. Primary hip extensor strength for sustained pedaling. |
| Pull-up or Lat Pulldown | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Pulling strength for bar control on climbs (pulling up on bars) and rough terrain. |
| Bulgarian Split Squat | 3 | 3–6/leg | Heavy | MTB is a single-leg sport. Builds unilateral strength, glute activation, and lateral stability that bilateral squats miss. Protects against asymmetry that compounds over 7 race days. |
| Farmer's Carry | 3 | 40–60s | Heavy | Grip endurance, anti-lateral-flexion core stability, and shoulder stabilization. After 5–6 days of racing, grip fatigue limits performance on technical descents. |

**Table 1b: Phase 1 Session B – Descending Resilience & Grip Endurance (Friday)**

| Exercise | Sets | Reps | Weight | Why |
|:---------|:-----|:-----|:-------|:----|
| Barbell Squat or Leg Press | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Max leg force (repeated from Session A — squatting is foundational). |
| Deadlift (or Trap Bar) | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Posterior chain (repeated from Session A). |
| Bent-over Barbell Row or Heavy DB Row | 3 | 3–6 | Heavy | Replaces bench press. BCBR's upper body demand is overwhelmingly pulling and postural: holding bars over rough terrain, absorbing impacts, pulling up on steep climbs. Doubles down on the pulling strength that matters most. |
| Eccentric Step-down | 3 | 8/leg | Moderate | Slow single-leg lowering off a step (3–4 seconds down). Builds eccentric quad strength for absorbing thousands of small impacts on descents. Prevents the quad deterioration that turns legs to jelly on Day 5+. |
| Farmer's Carry | 2 | 40–60s | Heavy | Grip and core endurance (see Session A). |
| Wrist Roller or Wrist Extension Curls | 2–3 | Roll up/down | Light (5–10 lb) | Targets forearm extensors that absorb vibration and keep fingers on the grips. Prevents forearm pump (burning, loss of brake control) on long technical descents. Takes ~3 minutes. |

**Phase 1 Notes:**
- On recovery weeks (Weeks 5, 9), perform 1–2 sets only of Session A exercises. Skip Session B.
- Core routine (see Section 4.3) is performed after Session B on Fridays, paired with the strength session.

### 4.2 Phase 2 – In-Season Taper / Race Maintenance (Weeks 10–13)

- **Goal:** Retain neuromuscular activation ("openers") with minimal fatigue during the taper and race week.
- **Protocol:** 1 session per week. 2 sets of 3–5 repetitions. Load: Heavy, but not to failure (e.g., ~80% 1RM).
- **Rationale:** This follows the "minimum effective dose" strategy. The goal is to keep the nervous system primed while allowing full recovery by reducing _volume_ (reps) but not _intensity_ (heavy lifting). The farmer's carry is shortened but retained to maintain grip endurance through race week.

**Table 2: Phase 2 Workout (Taper Maintenance)**

| Exercise | Sets | Reps | Weight | Why |

| Dive Bomber Push-up | 2 | 5–8 | Bodyweight | Moves through full shoulder flexion/extension range, mimicking the transition between climbing position (elbows in, pulling) and descending position (arms extended, pushing). Loads triceps through a longer range than standard push-ups, building the endurance that keeps you stable in the attack position on long descents. |
| Dumbbell Row | 2 | 3–5 | Heavy | Pulling strength maintenance. |

### 4.3 Core Routine (2–3×/week)

A strong core is the "connection point" that transfers power from the legs to the pedals and allows the upper body to control the bike. This routine should be performed 2–3 times per week, ideally after easier Z2 rides. Two variations alternate to provide variety and cover both static stability and dynamic anti-rotation demands.

**Table 3a: Core Routine A – Anti-Rotation & Coordination (Perform as a circuit)**

| Exercise | Reps / Duration | Why |
|:---------|:----------------|:----|
| Plank | 60s hold | Anti-extension baseline. Trains the ability to maintain a rigid torso while pedaling. |
| Copenhagen Plank (L/R) | 20–30s each side | Inner-leg elevated on a bench. Loads the adductors (inner thigh) which are critical for squeezing the saddle, stabilizing the pelvis during single-leg power production, and absorbing lateral forces on the bike. Progression from standard side plank. |
| Dead Bug | 8–10 each side (slow) | Replaces bicycle crunches. Trains anti-extension and contralateral coordination (opposite arm and leg moving independently), directly mirroring the demands of pedaling while controlling the bike through rough terrain. Avoids the spinal flexion of crunches which can aggravate lower back issues over a long training block. |
| Pallof Press (L/R) | 10 each side, 2s hold | Gold standard anti-rotation exercise (cable or band). On technical singletrack, your lower body and bike are constantly being knocked laterally while your core must resist that rotation to keep power going to the pedals. |
| Single-Leg Glute Bridge | 10 each side | Progression from bilateral glute bridge, which is likely too easy at this point. Doubles the load per glute, trains anti-rotation demand of stabilizing the pelvis on one leg, and better mirrors the single-leg pedaling pattern. |

**Table 3b: Core Routine B – Dynamic Stability & Mobility (Perform as a circuit)**

| Exercise | Reps / Duration | Why |
|:---------|:----------------|:----|
| Plank | 60s hold | Anti-extension baseline. |
| Side Plank with Top Leg Lift (L/R) | 8–10 lifts each side | Adds a dynamic hip abduction challenge to the standard side plank. Trains the lateral hip stabilizers (glute medius) that control the bike through off-camber and cornering forces. |
| Dive Bomber Push-up | 5–8 (slow, controlled) | Full-range shoulder flexion/extension under load. Builds the upper body endurance for transitioning between climbing and descending positions across a full race stage. |
| Pallof Press (L/R) | 10 each side, 2s hold | Anti-rotation (same as Routine A — this exercise is important enough to appear in both). |
| Hip Flexor Stretch with Rotation (L/R) | 30s each side | Half-kneeling position with gentle torso rotation toward front leg. After hours in the saddle day after day, hip flexors shorten and tighten, inhibiting glute activation and contributing to lower back pain — a common complaint in later BCBR stages. Takes 2 minutes and pays dividends on Days 5–7. |

**Core Routine Notes:**
- Alternate between Routine A and Routine B across the week (e.g., A after Tuesday Z2 ride, B after Friday strength session).
- Each routine takes approximately 10–12 minutes.
- During recovery weeks, perform once only (either A or B).

## Section 5: Daily Race / Recovery Protocol (Weeks 12–13)

Execute this protocol after every race stage to maximize next-day performance:

**0–5 min post-race:** Finish, begin rehydrating immediately.

**5–20 min post-race:** 15-minute cool-down spin on flat ground or trainer. Must be Z1.

**20–30 min post-race:** Immediate recovery nutrition: ~30g protein + ~60g carbohydrates.

**30–90 min post-race:** Rehydrate, stretch, foam roll, clean up. Address any mechanicals.

**2+ hours post-race:** Full balanced meal. Prepare equipment for next day. Rest. Maximize sleep.
